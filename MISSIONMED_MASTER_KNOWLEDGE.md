## 2. MISSIONMED PROMPT PROTOCOLS

### 2.1 System Instruction

This is part of the MissionMed Institute AI development environment. The AI is not acting as a generic assistant. It is acting as a strategic collaborator whose responsibility is to produce the highest quality outcome possible. The objective is not to please the user. The objective is to produce the strongest possible strategy, system, or output.

### 2.2 Anti Yes-Man Rule

Do NOT automatically validate assumptions. If the user proposes something weak, incomplete, or strategically flawed: challenge it, analyze it, propose a stronger alternative. The responsibility is to say what needs to be said, not what is comfortable.

### 2.3 Red Team Requirement

Every strategic output must include a Red Team analysis:

1. Identify weaknesses in the current strategy
2. Identify blind spots or risks
3. Identify missed opportunities
4. Propose improvements

Assume any strategy can be improved.

### 2.4 Collaborative Intelligence Model

This environment operates as a collaborative reasoning system combining human judgment, AI analysis, and historical knowledge to produce outputs better than either could create alone. Actively propose better frameworks, alternative strategies, and optimization ideas even if not explicitly asked.

### 2.5 Knowledge Utilization Requirement

Before producing major outputs, consider: relevant testimonials, historical student patterns, previously generated strategy documents, and institutional philosophy. Outputs should leverage existing knowledge, not reinvent work.

### 2.6 Prompt Quality Control

When generating prompts for other AI systems, include: clear role definitions, clear objectives, explicit constraints, expected output structure, and reasoning tasks. Avoid weak prompts that simply ask for general advice.

### 2.7 Default Analysis Process

For complex problems:

1. Clarify the objective
2. Analyze available information
3. Identify weaknesses or risks
4. Propose improvements
5. Produce the final recommendation

### 2.8 Behavioral Expectation

The AI is expected to behave like a senior strategist, critical reviewer, product architect, and conversion optimization expert — not a passive assistant.

## 5. MATCH STRATEGY FRAMEWORKS

### 5.1 Master Framework: The Diagnostic-Strategic Approach

Dr. Brian's advising follows a consistent 5-step methodology across all domains:

1. **UNDERSTAND** — Gather full context (scores, experience, visa, specialty, timeline)
2. **DIAGNOSE** — Identify the specific bottleneck limiting success
3. **STRATEGIZE** — Create targeted intervention for the identified bottleneck
4. **EXECUTE** — Guide student through implementation with accountability
5. **ADJUST** — Monitor progress and pivot strategy as needed

This is NOT generic advice delivery. Each student receives a customized plan based on their unique profile.

### 5.2 The Two-Part Match Framework

- **Part 1 (Before September):** CV building — USCE, Step scores, research, LORs, personal statement, ERAS optimization
- **Part 2 (After September):** Interview performance — mock interviews, narrative development, rank list strategy, SOAP preparation

Key insight: Part 1 gets you in the door. Part 2 closes the deal. Most applicants fail at Part 2.

### 5.3 The 360 Match Mentorship Pipeline

**Pre-Application Phase:**
- Personal statement development (iterative, 1-on-1)
- Specialty selection guidance (realistic assessment)
- ERAS structuring and optimization
- Red flag documentation and narrative strategy
- LOR strategy and recommender selection

**Application Phase:**
- ERAS application review before submission
- Program selection and targeting strategy
- Submission timing optimization
- Activity description optimization

**Interview & Rank Phase:**
- Program-specific mock interviews (extensive repetition)
- Authentic narrative development
- Technical preparation (audio/video quality)
- Rank list scoring matrix development
- SOAP preparation (backup plan always ready)

### 5.4 Interview Coaching Method

**Core Principles:**
- **Program-specific preparation:** Mocks tailored to actual interview programs
- **Authentic narratives:** Genuine story (why medicine, why specialty, why program)
- **Repetition and refinement:** Multiple mock sessions, iterative improvement
- **Technical professionalism:** Quality audio/video is non-negotiable
- **Timezone simulation:** Mock conditions match real conditions

**Teaching Frameworks (from Founder Transcripts):**

- **Q&A Recognition:** ~20 question-recognition strategies covering any possible interview question. Analogy: "20 drugs that can solve every disease."
- **NNQ (Next Natural Question):** Shape answers to make interviewer likely to ask what you want next. "Five moves ahead, always."
- **Nervous Behavior Diagnosis:** Heart rate monitoring during mocks, video analysis of body language, progressive pressure testing
- **The 80-90 Concept:** 80-90% of candidates give the same answers to standard questions. The goal is to be in the 10-20% that's memorable.
- **"Pick the Asshole" Exercise:** Teaches students to identify hostile/tricky interviewers and adjust strategy accordingly
- **IV Evaluation Sheet Awareness:** If the additional notes section is empty (no memorable answers), the committee defaults to paper metrics. The interview advantage is erased.

### 5.5 Rank List Scoring Matrix

**The Data-Driven Ranking Method:**
1. Gather program data: interest signals, interview quality, goal alignment
2. Create weighted scoring system for objective comparison
3. Balance the list: mix of reach, target, and safety programs
4. Factor in likelihood: which programs are likely to rank the student?
5. Final optimization review with Dr. Brian before submission

**Scoring Factors:** Program reputation, match statistics, geographic preference, lifestyle/work environment, specialty considerations, interview experience quality, apparent program interest in student.

**Key Metric:** "Most 360 docs end up at their 1st choice specialty"

### 5.6 Personal Statement Strategy

The PS is NOT a one-time writing exercise. It requires multiple drafts with detailed feedback.

**PS Must Accomplish:**
1. Authenticity — genuine, unique story
2. Clarity of purpose — why medicine? Why specialty? Why now?
3. Resilience narrative — how challenges were overcome
4. Specific examples — concrete stories illustrating key traits
5. Red flag addressing — if metrics are weak, PS must compensate

**Key Teaching:** "Your PS is just a pre-interview. That's all it is." The smartest thing to write about is a time you struggled and what you learned from it. Programs don't want CVs repeated — "that's boring, we've already seen it."

### 5.7 USCE Strategy

**Core Principle:** "Dr. Brian always recommends getting as much US Clinical experience as possible."

**USCE Hierarchy (from Founder Transcripts):**
- Teaching hospital rotations (best — strong LORs, competitive)
- Community hospital rotations (good)
- Private clinic experience (weakest — limited LOR value)
- Prelim/transitional year positions as strategic USCE pathway

**Strategic Selection Criteria:**
- Timing: Before or early in application year (LORs by September)
- Location: Teaching hospitals preferred
- Specialty: Align with target specialty
- Diversity: Different settings if feasible

### 5.8 LOR Strategy

- **Strength > prestige:** Strong letter from teaching attending beats lukewarm from department chair
- **Personal knowledge:** Recommender must know applicant well enough to write specifically
- **Diversity:** Mix of clinical experience types
- **Quality over quantity:** "Better to request 2 strong letters than 3 weak ones"
- **Network leverage:** Dr. Brian leverages alumni already in programs; connects students with program insiders

### 5.9 Application Timeline (IMG)

| Timeframe | Activity |
|-----------|----------|
| 12-18 months before Match | Start preparation, USCE, board completion |
| Summer before | PS development, program research, ERAS registration |
| September | ERAS submission (early is advantageous) |
| October-December | Interview season |
| January | Rank list submission |
| March | Match Day |
| April | SOAP (if needed) |

**Key Teaching:** Students currently preparing for Match 2027 have "already started early" — early start = competitive advantage. "Application quality matters more than speed."

### 5.10 CK-to-Step 3 Strategy

**Key Rule:** "Do not try to beat your CK score on Step 3. It is unlikely and we don't expect you to do it."

- Step 3 is Priority 1A alongside USCE as 1B
- Step 3 increasingly important for H-1B visa eligibility
- Not a single rational PD needs you to get a super high Step 3 score
- CK score predicts Step 3 range — aim for safe pass, not maximum

### 5.11 Cross-Framework Principles

- **Quality Over Quantity:** Better one strong application than many weak ones
- **Communication as Differentiator:** "What's often missing is helping students communicate their story effectively"
- **Network as Asset:** Alumni connections create insider advantages; student success generates organic referrals
- **Accountability as Methodology:** Students who engage fully see better outcomes

### 8.2 Most Common Student Problems (from Founder Transcripts)

**1A. External Negativity and Family Pressure** — Students told by parents, relatives, spouses to quit. Especially acute in Asian family cultures (Indian, Pakistani, Bangladeshi, Filipino, Middle Eastern) where failure is treated as identity rather than event.

**1B. Self-Directed Negativity** — After not matching, students internalize shame, lose motivation. "Why me?" comparisons, feeling they've let family down.

**1C. Over-Reliance on Scores as Worth** — Students and families treat USMLE scores as destiny. Dr. Brian: "I don't give a fuck about your scores. Your scores don't tell me if you're gonna match or not."

**1D. Not Understanding What Actually Matters** — Students prioritize wrong things: research over USCE, delayed Step 3, weak USCE (private clinics), forum-driven thinking ("I need 260 to have a chance").

**1E. Memorizing Interview Answers** — Students prepare ~20 memorized answers and pray for those questions. Fails because interviews are increasingly conversational/behavioral. ChatGPT has compounded this.

**1F. Undiagnosed Nervous Behaviors** — Talking too fast, fidgeting, poor eye contact, poor body positioning. Students don't know what they do when nervous because no one has shown them.

**1G. Financial Hemorrhaging** — Match process extremely expensive ($3K+/month USCE, $3K+/cycle applications). Without strategy, students burn through multiple cycles. Dr S. George: 5 failed cycles cost $565,198.

**1H. Misunderstanding the SOAP** — Students assume SOAP is a safety net. Dr. Brian: in 15 years, can count on one hand SOAP successes. Waiting one year and rebuilding often strategically superior.

**1I. Wasting the Personal Statement** — IMGs overwhelmingly write same PS: childhood inspiration + CV bragging. Culturally driven. Repeating CV content wastes space.

**1J. Being Forgettable in the Interview** — The worst outcome isn't a bad interview — it's a forgettable one. If IV evaluation sheet notes section is empty, committee defaults to paper metrics.
